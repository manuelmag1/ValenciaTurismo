Traditional Dishes

From the world-famous Paella to our sweet Horchata. Discover the historic flavors that define our vivid and delicious Mediterranean cuisine.

Authentic Paella

Born in the fields of Albufera, true Valencian paella is a masterpiece of Mediterranean cuisine. This iconic dish features tender rabbit and chicken, fresh flat green beans (judías verdes), and garrofó beans (a local variety), all slowly cooked together in a wide, shallow pan called a "paellera" over a crackling wood fire.

The magic of paella lies in the socarrat - that crispy, golden layer of rice that forms at the bottom of the pan. Each region of Valencia has its own variation, but the authentic Valencian paella reflects centuries of agricultural tradition and the bounty of the surrounding wetlands.

Today, paella remains not just a dish, but a symbol of Valencian identity, bringing families and communities together for celebrations and shared meals.

Fideuà de Gandia

Fideuà is a beloved variation of paella that replaces rice with short, thin noodles, creating a unique texture and flavor profile. This seafood-focused dish originated in the coastal town of Gandia and represents the marriage of Mediterranean bounty with Spanish culinary tradition.

Fresh seafood such as shrimp, mussels, squid, and fish are cooked together with the noodles in a rich, aromatic broth infused with saffron and garlic. The result is a delightful dish that bursts with the flavors of the sea, with a texture that combines the tenderness of the noodles with the succulence of the seafood.

Like paella, fideuà is best enjoyed with family and friends, served directly from the pan to create an authentic Mediterranean dining experience.

Horchata & Fartons

Horchata is Valencia's most beloved sweet treat and a tradition that dates back centuries. This refreshing beverage is made from tiger nuts (chufas), a small tuber native to Valencia that has been cultivated here since the 9th century. The nuts are ground, mixed with water and sugar, and filtered to create a creamy, slightly sweet drink.

Horchata is traditionally served ice-cold, often on warm summer afternoons, and is perfectly accompanied by fartons - long, decorative pastries that are meant for dipping. These soft, slightly sweet pastries have a light glaze and are the ideal complement to the creamy horchata.

The pairing of horchata and fartons is so iconic that it's declared a Protected Designation of Origin (PDO) product when made in Valencia. It's a simple pleasure that captures the essence of Valencian hospitality and sweet living.
